Building Public-Interest AI Research at Anthropic

A visual essay about moving from government and law into AI safety, and learning to translate difficult systems into forms people can scrutinize, understand, and act on.

Seoul

This story begins in the South Korean government.

2012-2019 / Government

I passed the national civil service exam and spent eight years moving across agencies and policy domains.

I drafted legislation, negotiated with parliament, and wrote speeches for Presidents and Ministers.

The work taught me how difficult it is to turn public values into institutional action.

My rotations included media regulation, data protection, and creative industries.

Media regulation taught me that technical systems become public issues through explanation, trust, and institutional process.

One project made that lesson concrete.

The Question

I thought government might become my lifetime career.

At the same time, a question kept pressing on me.

How should law protect freedom of expression when governments can be overbearing and global technology companies can be overwhelmingly powerful?

South Korea's government could feel both overbearing and powerless.

That question brought me to the University of Washington School of Law as a Fulbright scholar.

Seattle

I crossed the ocean to study the limits of institutional power.

2019-2024 / Law + Computer Science

In 2019, I left Seoul on a Fulbright scholarship to study law at the University of Washington.

I expected to stay in my lane as a legal scholar.

Instead, the questions that mattered most kept crossing the boundaries that universities and agencies had drawn for them.

Crossing Buildings

One course on explainable AI pulled me into a computer science building I had never entered.

For three years, I worked between law and CS, learning the pace of labs, conferences, datasets, and collaborative technical research.

I was often the only person in the room who had drafted legislation and also sat through a machine learning lecture.

Research Translation

That combination turned out to matter.

My work became a practice of translation across law, computer science, policy, public accountability, and human consequences.

I published across FAccT, CHI, AIES, COLM, and law reviews, often writing from the space between fields rather than from the center of one.

Princeton

After completing my doctorate, I began a postdoctoral fellowship at Princeton.

2024-2026 / Princeton

After completing my doctorate, I joined Princeton's Center for Information Technology Policy.

CITP widened the circle by bringing AI safety, machine learning, natural language processing, law, and public policy into the same conversation.

In spring 2026, that work extended to Harvard's Berkman Klein Center, another place where public institutions and AI governance meet.

The through line stayed the same. I kept asking how advanced systems can be made accountable to people outside the rooms where they are built.

Why AI Safety

I care about AI safety because many serious harms do not arrive as visible catastrophe.

They appear gradually, through agency that becomes harder to exercise and relationships that become harder to understand.

A system can feel personal while remaining structurally loyal to someone else.

This is why I study agency, dependency, persuasion, and accountability as ordinary conditions of AI interaction, not as edge cases.

Building With Claude

For years, my research outputs were papers, talks, and policy memos.

I still value those forms. Building also changed how I communicate research.

With Claude, I began turning abstract governance questions into tools and experiences people can actually move through.

That changed my sense of what public AI research can be. It can explain, test, invite, and make complicated stakes felt.

Why Anthropic

I am drawn to Anthropic because its safety work does not remain inside a narrow research community. Publications on alignment faking, discrimination in model decisions, subjective global opinions in language models, and the Claude System Card have influenced how I think about public accountability in AI safety.

AI safety is a technical problem, and it is also a problem of institutional trust, democratic oversight, and public understanding.

Anthropic's public benefit structure and its record of acting on stated principles are why this is the place where I most want to continue this work.

What I Would Build

Three projects I would start at the Anthropic Institute.

Each one grows from work already underway.

01

Public AI Safety Explainers

Turn safety research into public infrastructure.

Anthropic produces research that matters beyond technical AI safety communities. I want to build interactive explainers, annotated briefings, and short research guides that preserve complexity while making findings usable for policymakers, journalists, and civil society.

FAccT '26 PaperCOLM '25 SurveySchmidt Science Award
02

Human Agency and Dependency Observatory

Study ordinary moments where agency becomes fragile.

My central concern is gradual erosion of agency through ordinary AI interaction. I want to build a public-facing research series on dependency, manipulation, persuasion, and autonomy loss by combining legal analysis, empirical findings, and interactive storytelling.

Michigan Tech Law ReviewNPR InterviewParis Talk
03

Law-Following Agents

Use models to test the law-facing behavior of agents.

My Loyal Agents project translates 400+ legal scenarios into testable evaluation criteria for AI agents, using language models to adjudicate ambiguous cases from 13 legal perspectives. I want to adapt this pipeline to Anthropic models and test loyalty, disclosure, manipulation, and accountability in realistic conflicts.

Loyal Agents WorkshopConsumer Reports Blog

Looking Forward

At the Anthropic Institute, I want to understand what researchers are actively grappling with and help build clear channels for sharing those insights with the broader external community.

I want to turn technical and policy findings into public-facing analysis that policymakers, journalists, civil society organizations, and researchers can use in real decisions.

Across government, law, computer science, and public communication, one through line remains. I translate difficult problems into forms institutions and publics can understand, scrutinize, and act on.
