I am a legal scholar working across constitutional law, AI governance, and frontier AI evaluation, with a PhD in Law from the University of Washington, a postdoctoral appointment at Princeton's Center for Information Technology Policy, and a concurrent fellowship at Harvard's Berkman Klein Center. The through-line of my work is one problem. When AI systems grow powerful enough to influence legal judgment, public institutions, and democratic participation, governance has to be technically grounded, legally durable, and institutionally realistic at once. Most work manages one or two of those. I have spent my career trying to hold all three.

Anthropic's case for preserving the option of a credible slowdown is, to me, the hardest and most important of these problems, and the one I am most prepared to work on. A slowdown is a governance problem more than a technical one. Detecting training runs is the easy part. The harder questions are what triggers restraint, who verifies compliance, which actors gain by defecting, and what enforcement holds when the payoff from defecting in secret is enormous. Beneath those questions sit several structural failures that any real slowdown has to clear at once.

Consumers will not slow down on their own, because the individual payoff from automation is too large for anything like a cord-cutting moment to form. There is no substitute to switch to, and the product runs from a single API call to open weights to a closed interface, so no one control reaches all of it. The risks are hard to see coming, since reviewing weights or code reveals little about downstream effects and many vulnerabilities surface only after wide stress-testing in the wild, which makes some early exposure the price of learning where the danger sits. Regulation asks the labs with a frontier lead to give that lead up, and even when one or two do it on principle, the arrangement holds only with multistakeholder backing that no one has built. Liability, the oldest tool, strains here too, because the causal chain from a model to a harm is long and tangled, and putting the cost on a single developer does not obviously push the model toward safer behavior.

I learned to take these failures seriously in government, not in a seminar. For eight years I was a Deputy Director at the Korea Communications Commission and the Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism, drafting legislation, running regulatory programs, coordinating expert committees, and building national R&D strategy for emerging technologies. The lesson that stayed with me is that a sound principle is the easy part. The work is aligning incentives, building procedures people will actually use, sustaining cooperation past the first disagreement, and designing enforcement that survives political and operational stress. When the benefits of a rule are spread wide and the costs land on a few, free-riding becomes the main event. When participants see no clear gain and no clear penalty for walking away, collective governance falls apart even when everyone endorses the goal.

My dissertation, Collaborative Approaches to AI Governance, studied this directly. One case study looked at co-design in legal AI through case-based deliberation with legal experts. The other compared co-regulation in South Korea's web comics and news industries. The web comics sector held a durable arrangement because creators, platforms, and regulators were interdependent and had clear reasons to stay at the table. The news sector could not, because trust was low, incentives were fragmented, and each side claimed institutional independence. A multi-lab slowdown faces the same test at higher stakes. It cannot run on aspiration. It needs a worked-out map of the incentives facing frontier labs, governments, smaller actors that AI now empowers, and adversarial states, and a realistic account of the transparency, verification, and enforcement that differently situated actors would actually accept.
This is the contribution I am readiest to make. Anthropic's proposal to keep a slowdown option open is timely and, in a field racing ahead, unusually public-spirited. Making it real takes more than verification tools. It takes a co-regulatory arrangement that can specify triggers, name who adjudicates, guard against strategic defection, and make participation rational for actors who do not share values or threat models. My regulatory experience and my study of collaborative governance let me start on that immediately.

My fieldwork changed how I think about AI and democratic institutions. At Princeton CITP I led the first interview study of how public defenders adopt AI in daily practice. The public conversation fixes on hallucinated citations and the trust they erode, a real concern. The defenders I interviewed pointed elsewhere. Their binding constraints are case backlog, resource scarcity, and the sheer breadth of public defense. The work includes reviewing hundreds of hours of body-worn camera footage to find one usable detail, translating legal jargon for frightened clients who may not speak English, arranging community resources for a client's children while the client is incarcerated, and making strategic calls under crushing time pressure.

That work showed me that the public defender's office cannot be read only as a site of legal reasoning. It is a constitutional institution and a welfare institution and a communication institution, and often the last point of contact between the state and a very vulnerable person. Governance aimed only at suppressing hallucinated citations misses the tools that would help defenders now and strengthen the Sixth Amendment in practice, locally deployed video analysis, retrieval built on real defender queries, secure summarization, language-access support, anything that lifts administrative load without displacing professional judgment. The analysis I want to bring is grounded in where institutions already fail, where AI could help, and where it could erode the functions we are trying to keep. The same lens reaches courts, administrative agencies, and legislatures.

The study also produced a build. With the New Jersey Office of the Public Defender, our team is developing BriefBank, a retrieval system that lets appellate defenders search prior briefs and reuse arguments, precedent, and winning strategy. I led the practitioner needs assessment and produced the legal-domain annotations for the retrieval benchmark. We found that systems trained or evaluated on artificial legal datasets miss what defenders search for on the job, which is the methodological point under all my work. Governance should start from professional practice and institutional constraint, not from abstract benchmarks.

My loyalty work turns legal doctrine into evaluation. At Consumer Reports I lead a project on AI agent loyalty that uses Anthropic's Bloom pipeline to generate realistic agent-user interaction traces, and evaluates whether agents breach duties of loyalty adapted from the Restatement (Third) of Agency. This sits close to the legal-alignment lens in the posting. The hard part comes after getting a model to behave, in deciding what counts as a violation, where to source seed scenarios, how to generate variations that are legally significant, and how to write criteria faithful to doctrine that an engineer can still apply. We collected more than 400 scenarios drawn from human agent failures and adapted them to AI agent contexts, and we now work with attorneys in deliberative workshops to define the modifiers that create those variations. One early finding guides the method. Lawyers, drawing on clinical and professional intuition, generate more varied and surprising edge cases than a model does on its own, which has moved us toward keeping lawyers in the loop for scenario generation and red-teaming. Legal norms do not reduce to slogans. They have to be operationalized through scenarios, adversarial facts, institutional roles, and failure modes.

I also study where law does not translate into AI safety rules. Agency law's liability rules assume a human agent who forms intentions, exercises professional judgment, and can be disciplined by a court or a licensing board, and AI agents unsettle each assumption. First Amendment doctrine has no clean slot for an AI provider that is neither an individual editor nor a neutral tool maker, since these systems exercise discretion and mediate access to knowledge while responding dynamically to users. My current work with ethicists and computer scientists asks how doctrines built on human mental states break down when a court tries to assign product liability for a model's output. These are not abstractions. They will decide how responsibility is assigned, how regulators define permissible intervention, and how companies design safer systems.

Much of the value in this work sits in the friction between law and engineering. In an early HCI study funded through OpenAI's Democratic Inputs program, I looked at whether AI chatbots should give legal advice to lay users. We expected attorneys to resist, and most instead saw a way to widen access to legal information while holding a firm line between legal information and legal opinion. That line is obvious to lawyers and slippery to technologists, so we spelled out legal opinion for a computer science audience as choosing the most defensible next move among several options, or applying a rule to a specific fact pattern to predict a likely outcome. The translation is slow, and it is where most of the value lies.

None of my projects has moved fast. The loyalty work has meant weekly sessions with Consumer Reports engineers for over a year. With public defenders, it meant earning the trust of practitioners with good reason to be wary of AI research. The legal-advice study meant designing deliberation that would draw out tacit professional judgment rather than abstract opinion. Cross-disciplinary governance takes more than gathering people from different fields in a room. It takes designed processes, case-based workshops, annotation handbooks, shared scoring criteria, and conversations that run long enough for real disagreement to surface.

I would help build the team's agenda along several lines, legal-alignment evaluations for frontier models and agents, institutional vulnerability studies of courts, public defense, administrative agencies, and legislatures, governance analysis of credible slowdown mechanisms, and research on how AI companies should be treated as institutions under constitutional and regulatory law. I would also use Claude as an object of inquiry, testing how it reasons about due process, authority, loyalty, legal uncertainty, procedural fairness, and institutional role constraints.

My work has stayed in the space this role requires. I have written legal scholarship on constitutional law, free speech, agency, and liability, published in computer science venues, built LLM-based research tools, worked alongside practicing lawyers and engineers and civil society, and served inside government where the difficulty of regulation is concrete. Democratic institutions will not only be affected by AI from the outside. They will depend on it, regulate it, and be judged by the public through how they use it. The work is to keep that dependence from hollowing out the practices that make these institutions legitimate, and I would be glad to take it on.
